The fact that all the people pictured here are men is also problematic, but that's not what I'm here to talk about. In this talk I want to talk about some other problems - the mistakes that we make as leaders.
Sometimes as a technical lead, you want to do what you're good at. For most leads, we've been promoted after demonstrating our competence as effective individual contributors. , you get satisfaction from solving problems - building things, writing code, fixing bugs. As an effective individual contributor, you want to be in the thick of things, getting things done. When you see your team struggling, you’re tempted to jump in and do it. It’s quicker just to do it yourself. But it's not scalable, and it doesn't leave space for the team to learn It's nice for your ego to be treated like a hero. It's not scalable, and it's not good for your health. The team don't get a chance to learn. This is the mistake that I most frequently make. Some leaders believe that information is power, and want to hoard it to maximise their power.
If not having enough information is a problem, having too much noise coming at the team. Value people’s time - don’t involve them unless they need to be involved Meetings are expensive, even when working remotely. Perhaps even more so when working remotely One of the best project managers I ever worked with had the very valuable skill of shielding the team from noise. He protected them from clients where necessary. He did his job, trusted his team, and got out of their way so that they could get on with their jobs.

Sometimes technical leads can see themselves as gatekeepers of the code, keeping quality high, reviewing every line for potential problems. An obsession with quality can slow down progress. Sometimes we might need to remind ourselves that our clients aren't paying for elegant code, they're paying for working systems.
On the other hand, it's not OK to just ship any old rubbish. We need to encourage our teams to care about quality.
So you've probably noticed a theme running through these slides, and I'm definitely not the first person to propose it. It's the Goldilocks principle - not too hot or too cold, but just right. It's the golden mean. It's Buddhism's Middle Way. There are other axes we could think of It's assertive communication, as opposed to passive or aggressive In almost every aspect of life, and of leadership, taking an extreme path is likely to lead to problems.
